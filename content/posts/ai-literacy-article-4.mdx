Article 4 of the EU AI Act requires providers and deployers of AI systems to take
measures to ensure a **sufficient level of AI literacy** among their staff and anyone
operating AI systems on their behalf. The obligation has applied since February 2025.

The problem? The regulation does not define "sufficient." That ambiguity is a feature,
not a bug — but it leaves organisations unsure of what a defensible programme actually
looks like.

## What "sufficient" actually means

The level of literacy required is **proportionate to the role**. A board member needs a
different depth of understanding than an engineer fine-tuning a model. A defensible
programme accounts for this by tailoring content to each audience:

- **Executives and decision-makers** — enough to understand obligations, risk exposure,
  and accountability.
- **Legal and compliance teams** — the regulatory framework in depth, classification
  logic, and documentation requirements.
- **Technical teams** — how specific obligations map onto system design, data handling,
  and monitoring.

## Document everything

Whatever programme you run, **record it**. Attendance, content, dates, and the rationale
for the level chosen. If a regulator asks how you have met Article 4, the answer should
be a folder, not a shrug.
